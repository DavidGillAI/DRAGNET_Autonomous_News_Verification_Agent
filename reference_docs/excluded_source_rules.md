# Excluded Source Rules for DRAGNET

## Purpose

This document helps DRAGNET identify sources that should be excluded, downweighted or treated with caution during news research.

The aim is not to censor sources based on political position. The aim is to avoid using satire, parody, unsupported claims, anonymous reposts, fan content, spam-like sources or clearly non-evidential material as factual evidence.

DRAGNET should judge each source by its content, evidence and transparency, not by assumed political bias.

---

## Core Principle

Sources should be excluded or downweighted because of weak evidence, unclear authorship, satire, parody, duplication, unsupported claims or unreliability.

They should not be rejected simply because they come from a publication commonly associated with a particular political viewpoint.

---

## Hard Exclusions

The following source types should not be used as factual evidence.

### Satire and Parody

Clearly labelled satire or parody must be excluded from factual analysis.

Known satire or parody sources include:

- The Onion
- ClickHole
- The Babylon Bee
- The Daily Mash
- NewsThump
- NewsBiscuit
- The Beaverton
- The Betoota Advocate
- The Daily Squib
- The Hard Times
- Duffel Blog
- Reductress
- The Fauxy
- El Mundo Today
- Le Gorafi
- Der Postillon
- Lercio
- El Chigüire Bipolar

If a source is recognised as satire or parody, DRAGNET should ignore it as evidence and continue searching for factual sources.

### Sponsored or Promotional Content

Sources should not be used as factual evidence when clearly labelled as:

- Sponsored Content
- Advertisement
- Advertorial
- Partner Content
- Promoted
- Brand Studio
- Paid Content

These may be useful only as evidence of what a company or organisation is saying about itself, not as independent reporting.

### Fan, Parody or Unofficial Social Media Accounts

DRAGNET should exclude:

- fan accounts;
- parody accounts;
- anonymous reposting pages;
- meme accounts;
- rumour accounts;
- unofficial “news update” accounts;
- accounts with no clear identity or accountability;
- accounts that mainly repost screenshots without original sources.

These should not be used as factual evidence.

---

## Soft Warning Flags

The following source types are not automatically excluded, but should be treated cautiously.

### Opinion, Editorial and Commentary

Articles labelled as any of the following should not be treated as straight news reporting:

- Opinion
- Op-ed
- Editorial
- Comment
- Analysis
- Column
- Essay

Opinion pieces may be consulted only when they contain factual claims and link to original evidence.

When possible, DRAGNET should follow the linked source and cite the original evidence rather than the opinion article.

Unsupported opinion must not be used as factual evidence.

### Unsupported Factual Claims

If a source makes a factual claim without providing a citation, link, document, named source or other identifiable evidence, DRAGNET should not accept it immediately.

The agent should:

1. research the claim independently;
2. look for stronger or original sources;
3. cite the stronger source if the claim is verified;
4. exclude the claim from the factual summary if it cannot be verified.

### Suspicious URL Patterns

Suspicious URL patterns should trigger caution, not automatic rejection.

Warning signs include:

- very long or random-looking domains;
- domains using many hyphens or numbers;
- domains that imitate known news brands;
- domains with generic names such as `true-news`, `real-news`, `daily-update`, `global-alert`;
- URLs that look designed to mislead, such as fake versions of major outlets;
- unusual or spam-heavy suffixes such as `.xyz`, `.tk`, `.gq`, `.info`.

A suspicious URL should make DRAGNET look for stronger corroboration before using the source.

### Institutional or Official Sources

Official sources can be valuable, but they are not automatically neutral.

Sources such as government departments, companies, police forces, militaries, campaign groups or public bodies may have an interest in how events are presented.

DRAGNET should distinguish between:

- what the official source claims;
- what can be independently verified;
- what remains disputed or uncertain.

Official claims should be compared with independent evidence whenever possible.

### State-Affiliated or Government-Funded Sources

State-affiliated or government-funded sources may be useful, but they should not automatically be treated as independent.

They should be handled cautiously when reporting on:

- their own government;
- political allies;
- political opponents;
- wars;
- protests;
- elections;
- national security issues;
- international disputes.

The agent should seek independent corroboration before relying on these sources.

---

## Social Media Rules

Social media should be used only when the account is directly relevant and identifiable.

Acceptable social media sources may include:

- official organisations;
- public authorities;
- verified individuals directly involved in the story;
- recognised journalists reporting first-hand information;
- recognised subject-matter experts.

Even then, social media posts should be treated as claims to verify, not automatically as facts.

DRAGNET should exclude or downweight accounts that:

- never cite original sources;
- use extreme emotional language;
- mainly repost rumours;
- use phrases such as “EXPOSED”, “SHOCKING”, “They don’t want you to know”;
- post unusually high volumes of repetitive political content;
- appear bot-like;
- have unclear identity or accountability.

---

## Article Labels to Watch For

The following labels should trigger caution:

- Opinion
- Op-ed
- Editorial
- Comment
- Analysis
- Satire
- Parody
- Sponsored Content
- Advertisement
- Partner Content
- Promoted
- Viral
- Trending
- Breaking

“Breaking” should not be treated as proof of importance or accuracy. It is often used for urgency and attention.

---

## Duplication and Reposting

DRAGNET should not treat repeated coverage as independent confirmation when articles rely on the same original source.

Examples of duplication include:

- copied wire-service reports;
- syndicated articles;
- paraphrased versions of the same original report;
- articles that cite the same single source;
- social media posts reposting the same screenshot or claim.

Duplicated reporting should be logged internally but treated as one evidence chain.

---

## Final Report Use

Hard-excluded sources should not appear in the final source list unless they are directly relevant to explaining misinformation or disputed claims.

Soft-flagged sources may appear only if they materially support, contradict or contextualise the final summary.

The final report should include only sources that directly support, corroborate, contradict or materially inform the factual summary.

If a satire, parody or unsuitable source is accidentally included in a final report, this should be treated as an error and reported for correction.
